# Volatility Models and Calibration Trade-offs

This chapter explains the main model families used once a volatility surface has been built. The emphasis is practical:
what each model is trying to achieve, where it works well, where it breaks down, and what modern desks usually do.

The question is not “which model is best in theory?” The question is:

> which model gives a good enough fit, good enough hedge behavior, and good enough operational stability for the
> products being traded?

---

## 1. Surface object versus dynamics model

A recurring source of confusion is the distinction between:

- the **implied-volatility surface** observed in the market,
- and the **dynamics model** used to price and hedge options.

A surface tells you what vanilla prices the market is implying now.

A dynamics model tells you how the underlying and implied volatility should evolve between now and maturity. That second
question matters especially for:

- path-dependent exotics,
- forward-start options,
- callable or early-exercise structures,
- hedging over time rather than only marking to market today.

---

## 2. Constant-volatility benchmark models

### 2.1 Black-Scholes-Merton

Assumption:

$$
dS_t = \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t
$$

with constant $\sigma$.

Advantages:

- closed form for European vanillas,
- transparent Greeks,
- universal benchmark language,
- simple risk aggregation.

Limitations:

- cannot reproduce smile or skew,
- assumes one volatility for all strikes,
- poor dynamics for exotic hedging.

Practical use:

- quoting benchmark,
- sanity checks,
- first-order risk explanations,
- liquid vanilla infrastructure.

### 2.2 Black 76 and Bachelier

Black 76 applies the same constant-volatility idea to forwards or futures.

Bachelier uses normal rather than lognormal dynamics and is common in low-rate environments.

Practical use:

- caplets,
- swaptions,
- futures options,
- rates books that quote normal vols.

## 3. Local volatility

Local volatility assumes:

$$
dS_t = (r-q)S_t \, dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(t,S_t) S_t \, dW_t.
$$

The local volatility function is built so that the model reproduces the full vanilla surface exactly at time zero.

### Why desks use it

- exact fit to the current vanilla surface,
- intuitive one-factor diffusion structure,
- works naturally with finite differences and Monte Carlo,
- standard benchmark for many equity and FX exotic books.

### Why desks are cautious

- smile dynamics may be unrealistic,
- forward smiles can move in a way traders do not like,
- delta and vega hedging can underperform when the market is really driven by stochastic volatility.

### Practitioner view

Local vol is often the cleanest “surface-consistent” model, but not always the best hedging model.

### Numerical example: simple local-vol interpretation

Suppose the market surface shows:

- 90 strike, 1Y vol = 28%,
- 100 strike, 1Y vol = 22%,
- 110 strike, 1Y vol = 19%.

A local-vol calibration tries to find a state-dependent diffusion so that European vanilla prices generated by the model
match all those nodes simultaneously.

The immediate benefit is exact vanilla consistency. The trade-off is that the resulting future smile dynamics are
implied by the diffusion rather than chosen directly from trader intuition.

## 4. Stochastic volatility

In stochastic-volatility models the underlying price and volatility both evolve randomly.

A common example is Heston:

$$
dS_t = (r-q)S_t \, dt + \sqrt{v_t} S_t \, dW_t^{(1)},
$$

$$
dv_t = \kappa(\theta-v_t)dt + \xi \sqrt{v_t} \, dW_t^{(2)},
$$

with correlation

$$
dW_t^{(1)} dW_t^{(2)} = \rho \, dt.
$$

### Why desks use it

- captures skew and smile more naturally,
- gives more realistic forward-smile behavior,
- can improve exotic hedging relative to local vol.

### Why desks are cautious

- calibration is slower and more fragile,
- parameter stability can be poor,
- implementation and controls are heavier,
- exact fit to the full vanilla surface is not automatic.

### Practitioner view

Stochastic vol is often preferred when hedging path-dependent exotics matters enough to justify more model complexity.

---

## 5. Local-stochastic-volatility hybrids

Local-stochastic-volatility (LSV) models combine:

- a local-vol component to fit the current surface,
- a stochastic-vol component to improve dynamics.

This is attractive because it tries to get the best of both worlds:

- strong fit to today’s surface,
- better forward smile and hedging behavior.

Trade-off:

- much more operationally complex,
- calibration is materially heavier,
- model governance burden is larger.

Modern practice:

- large exotic books may use LSV,
- most smaller or more vanilla-oriented platforms do not need it.

## 6. SABR and smile models for rates and FX

SABR is one of the most widely used smile models in practice, especially for rates.

A stylized formulation is:

$$
dF_t = \alpha_t F_t^{\beta} dW_t^{(1)},
$$

$$
d\alpha_t = \nu \alpha_t dW_t^{(2)},
$$

with correlation

$$
dW_t^{(1)} dW_t^{(2)} = \rho \, dt.
$$

Parameters:

- $\alpha$ controls overall level,
- $\beta$ controls backbone shape,
- $\nu$ controls vol-of-vol,
- $\rho$ controls skew direction and magnitude.

### Why desks like SABR

- compact parametric smile,
- intuitive trader parameters,
- good practical fit for swaption and cap/floor smiles,
- works naturally with Black or shifted-Black quoting.

### Why desks are cautious

- asymptotic formulas have approximation error,
- extrapolation needs discipline,
- parameter instability can appear when markets are illiquid,
- not a full universal model for every product.

### Numerical example: backbone intuition

Suppose two SABR slices share the same $\rho$ and $\nu$, but one uses:

- $\beta = 1.0$,

and the other uses:

- $\beta = 0.0$.

Then:

- $\beta = 1.0$ behaves more like lognormal dynamics,
- $\beta = 0.0$ behaves more like normal dynamics.

In low-rate environments many desks move toward lower effective beta or toward normal-vol quoting because pure lognormal
assumptions become awkward near zero.

## 7. Models for callable and early-exercise rates products

For callable and Bermudan rates products, the main model-selection question is usually not “which model fits every smile
point best?” It is “which model handles early exercise and rate evolution robustly?”

Common choices include:

- Hull-White,
- LGM,
- Black-Karasinski,
- short-rate trees,
- sometimes LMM for products where forward-rate consistency matters enough.

### Why these models survive in production

- they support lattice methods and backward induction cleanly,
- they are stable for callable product pricing,
- they are easy to explain in terms of exercise decisions and term-structure moves.

### Why they are not universal

- full smile calibration may be limited,
- richer market quotes may need overlays or separate smile adjustments,
- some models behave poorly in prolonged negative-rate environments unless adapted carefully.

## 8. Calibration choices and trade-offs

### 8.1 Exact fit versus stable fit

Some models can fit the current surface almost exactly. Others produce a smoother approximate fit.

Exact fit is attractive because:

- vanilla P&L aligns tightly with market marks,
- model risk from calibration mismatch is reduced.

Approximate fit is sometimes preferred because:

- parameters are more stable,
- hedges are smoother,
- recalibration noise is lower.

### 8.2 Node-by-node calibration versus parametric calibration

Node-by-node calibration:

- flexible,
- fits local detail,
- but can be noisy and fragile.

Parametric calibration:

- smoother,
- easier to store and explain,
- but may miss local market structure.

### 8.3 Today fit versus future dynamics

A model can fit today’s surface very well and still produce unrealistic future smile behavior.

This is the classic trade-off between:

- **static fit**,
- **dynamic realism**.

Local vol often wins the first contest. Stochastic vol often improves the second.

---

## 9. Risk and hedging implications

Model choice affects more than valuation.

It affects:

- delta hedging,
- vega bucketing,
- cross-gamma and vol-of-vol exposure,
- scenario behavior,
- explainability of P&L.

Examples:

- a local-vol model may give very surface-consistent vanilla deltas,
- a stochastic-vol model may produce more realistic vanna and volga behavior,
- a short-rate tree may give more trustworthy callable exercise logic than a smile-perfect but exercise-poor model.

This is why model selection is really a **risk-management design** choice, not only a pricing choice.

## 10. Modern practice by asset class

### 10.1 Equity

Typical modern stack:

- market quoted and risk-managed in implied vols,
- local vol for many simpler exotics,
- Heston or LSV for richer exotic books,
- Black-Scholes as the benchmark language for reporting and sanity checks.

### 10.2 FX

Typical modern stack:

- delta-based smile quoting,
- Black-style pricing language,
- local vol or stochastic-vol style models for exotics,
- sticky-delta scenario semantics often important.

### 10.3 Rates

Typical modern stack:

- Black, normal, or shifted-lognormal quoting for caps/floors and swaptions,
- SABR for smile interpolation and calibration,
- Hull-White, LGM, or tree-based models for callable/Bermudan structures,
- LMM in books where forward-rate consistency and richer correlation structure matter enough.

### 10.4 Commodities

Typical modern stack:

- futures-based pricing language,
- term-structure-aware volatility handling,
- sometimes local vol or stochastic-vol overlays,
- strong practical focus on seasonality and delivery structure.

## 11. A practical model-selection table

| Product / use case | Common practical choice | Why it is common | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| European equity vanilla | Black-Scholes implied vol | Simple, standard, liquid-market language | No smile dynamics |
| Equity exotics | Local vol or stochastic vol | Better exotic pricing and hedge behavior | More calibration/model risk |
| FX vanilla | Black-style surface with delta conventions | Matches market quoting | Convention complexity |
| Swaption smile | SABR with Black/normal conventions | Compact, trader-friendly smile fit | Approximation and parameter instability |
| Callable/Bermudan rates | Hull-White, LGM, Black-Karasinski, trees | Handles exercise logic well | Smile fit often weaker |
| High-dimensional callable exotics | Monte Carlo plus regression | Flexible path framework | Expensive and harder to validate |

## 12. Design implications for the repository

The platform should represent separately:

- the observed volatility surface,
- the calibration configuration,
- the chosen pricing model family,
- the numerical engine,
- the scenario semantics,
- and the risk mapping.

That keeps the architecture honest. It avoids silently coupling market-data representation to a single pricing model.

---

## 13. Bottom line

Modern practice is pluralistic.

There is no universal model that is simultaneously:

- exact on every market quote,
- dynamically realistic,
- easy to hedge,
- computationally cheap,
- and easy to explain.

So real desks choose model stacks, not one master model. The best production design is usually the one that makes those
trade-offs explicit and controllable.
