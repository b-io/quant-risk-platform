# Global Macro, Trader Workflow, and How the Platform Helps

## 1. What global macro means

Global macro is a **top-down investment approach**.
The starting point is not a single company or security.
The starting point is the state of the economy, policy, liquidity, and geopolitics.

The basic chain is:

$$
\text{economy}
\rightarrow \text{policy expectations}
\rightarrow \text{market pricing}
\rightarrow \text{trade expression}
\rightarrow \text{risk / P\&L}
$$

A global macro trader asks questions such as:

- Is growth accelerating or slowing?
- Is inflation rising, falling, or sticky?
- Is the central bank behind the curve or ahead of it?
- Is the market already pricing too many cuts or too many hikes?
- Where is the cleanest instrument to express the view: rates, FX, credit, equity index, commodity, or options?

> Global macro is a top-down approach where the investor forms views on growth, inflation, monetary and fiscal policy,
> liquidity, and geopolitical developments, then expresses those views through liquid instruments across rates, FX,
> credit, commodities, and equity indices.

It is **not only long-term investing**.
Some macro positions last months, but many are tactical and react to incoming data, policy meetings, and repricing
events.

## 2. The macro state variables that matter most

A practical way to think about macro is with five state variables:

1. growth
2. inflation
3. policy
4. liquidity / financial conditions
5. risk sentiment / positioning

### 2.1 Growth

Growth means the speed of economic activity.

Typical evidence:

- GDP
- PMIs / ISM
- industrial production
- retail sales
- housing activity
- consumer confidence
- labor market data

In simple terms:

- strong growth tends to support higher yields, tighter credit spreads, stronger cyclicals,
- weak growth tends to support lower yields, wider credit spreads, and defensive positioning.

A simple macro mental model is:

$$
\text{stronger growth}
\Rightarrow \text{higher expected real activity}
\Rightarrow \text{higher policy path / higher yields, all else equal}
$$

### 2.2 Inflation

Inflation is the rate at which prices rise.

Main concepts:

- headline inflation
- core inflation
- goods vs. services inflation
- wage inflation
- inflation expectations

If inflation is persistent, central banks may keep policy tighter.
If inflation falls quickly, the market may price cuts.

Very roughly, nominal yields can be decomposed as:

$$
\text{nominal yield} \approx \text{real yield} + \text{expected inflation} + \text{term / risk premia}
$$

### 2.3 Policy

Policy is mainly the central-bank reaction function, plus fiscal policy.

The key idea is:

$$
\text{data} \rightarrow \text{expected policy path} \rightarrow \text{curve repricing}
$$

Macro traders watch:

- central-bank meetings
- speeches
- minutes
- updated projections
- fiscal announcements
- issuance and debt dynamics

> Macro traders do not only forecast the economy. They forecast how the central bank will react to the economy, and how
> the market will reprice that reaction.

### 2.4 Liquidity and financial conditions

Even with the same growth and inflation numbers, markets can behave differently depending on:

- funding conditions
- balance-sheet stress
- issuance pressure
- market depth
- dealer inventories
- broad risk appetite

This matters a lot in credit and rates because the same fundamental shock can produce very different price moves in
liquid vs. stressed markets.

### 2.5 Positioning and consensus

A view is not enough.
The trader must ask:

- Is this already fully priced?
- Is positioning crowded?
- What is the asymmetry if the next data point surprises?

That is why macro is not just economics.
It is economics **plus market pricing**.

## 3. The main principles that govern macro logic

## 3.1 Inflation, growth, and central-bank reaction

A very simplified macro map:

- **strong growth + sticky inflation** tends to push toward tighter policy, higher front-end yields, possibly stronger
  currency, pressure on duration-sensitive assets.
- **falling growth + falling inflation** tends to push toward easier policy, lower yields, support for duration, but
  maybe weaker cyclicals.
- **falling growth + sticky inflation** is the difficult regime: growth deteriorates but inflation does not fall enough.
  This can create curve twists, policy uncertainty, and cross-asset stress.
- **strong growth + falling inflation** can create a risk-on environment, tighter spreads, stronger equities, but the
  exact market effect depends on prior pricing.

A useful simple rule is:

$$
\text{asset move} = f(\text{data surprise}, \text{current pricing}, \text{positioning}, \text{policy reaction})
$$

The key is **surprise relative to what the market already expects**.

## 3.2 Recession logic

A recession usually means broad economic contraction.
Common macro consequences:

- lower growth expectations
- lower policy path expectations
- lower sovereign yields, especially if inflation is under control
- wider credit spreads
- weaker cyclicals / risk assets
- stronger demand for safe assets

But the exact effect depends on whether inflation is also falling.
If inflation remains too high, the usual “bad growth = lower yields” relationship can be weaker or delayed.

## 3.3 Curves matter, not only levels

In macro, the shape of the curve matters a lot.

A yield curve can:

- steepen
- flatten
- bull steepen
- bull flatten
- bear steepen
- bear flatten

In practice:

- **bull move** = yields fall
- **bear move** = yields rise
- **steepener** = long-end rises more or falls less than short-end, or short-end falls more than long-end depending on
  direction
- **flattener** = the opposite

The curve shape embeds expectations about policy, growth, and term premia.

## 3.4 Credit as macro transmission

Credit spreads reflect:

- default risk
- downgrade risk
- liquidity conditions
- growth outlook
- risk appetite

A weak macro outlook often widens spreads.
A benign macro outlook often tightens spreads.

A good one-line intuition is:

$$
\text{credit spread} \approx \text{compensation for default risk} + \text{liquidity premium} + \text{risk premium}
$$

## 4. How a global macro trader actually works

You remembered something important: traders often **wait for new data**, then analyze the deviation from expectation.
That is exactly part of the workflow.

A realistic simplified daily process is:

$$
\text{monitor}
\rightarrow \text{form / update view}
\rightarrow \text{choose trade expression}
\rightarrow \text{size risk}
\rightarrow \text{monitor P\&L and exposures}
\rightarrow \text{revise after new data}
$$

## 4.1 Before the data release

The trader asks:

- What is the market consensus?
- what is the internal expectation
- What is already priced in the curve / FX / spreads / vols?
- Which instruments are most sensitive to surprise?
- Where is liquidity best?
- what are the downside scenarios if the view is wrong

This is where scenario analysis matters.

## 4.2 At the data release

They compare:

$$
\text{surprise} = \text{actual} - \text{expected}
$$

But they also decompose:

- is the surprise broad-based or one-off?
- is it headline or core?
- is it quality growth or weak growth?
- is there revision risk in prior prints?
- how will central-bank speakers interpret it?

Then they ask:

$$
\text{Does this change the policy path enough to justify a repricing?}
$$

## 4.3 After the release

The trader watches:

- front-end rates
- long-end rates
- curve shape
- inflation breakevens
- FX reaction
- credit spread reaction
- equity index and vol reaction

They try to identify whether the move is:

- consistent with the macro logic,
- overdone,
- underdone,
- or contaminated by positioning / liquidity.

## 4.4 Choosing the trade expression

A macro view can be expressed many ways.
For example, a view that inflation will stay higher than expected can be expressed via:

- short front-end bonds,
- pay fixed in swaps,
- receive inflation-linked exposure,
- long a currency with relatively tighter policy,
- options if the main view is on volatility of repricing.

The expression depends on:

$$
\text{best trade} = \arg\max \text{(conviction, liquidity, asymmetry, carry, risk-adjusted payoff)}
$$

This is not a literal optimization in most discretionary shops, but it is the right conceptual framing.

## 4.5 Ongoing risk management

After the position is on, the trader needs:

- live P\&L
- exposures by factor
- scenario and stress results
- event calendar awareness
- explain of what moved the book

This is where the quant developer becomes critical.

## 5. What the trader monitors

A macro trader usually monitors five layers at once.

### 5.1 Economic calendar

They watch releases such as:

- CPI / PPI
- payrolls / unemployment
- GDP
- PMIs
- retail sales
- central-bank decisions
- bond auctions
- fiscal announcements

### 5.2 Market-implied state

They monitor:

- yield levels
- curve slopes
- forward paths
- FX forwards and carry
- credit spreads / CDS spreads
- option-implied volatility
- breakeven inflation

### 5.3 P\&L and exposures

They want to know:

- where did the book make or lose money?
- what is the DV01 by bucket?
- what is the CS01 by sector / tenor?
- what is the exposure to inflation / vol / FX?
- what is the carry and roll profile?

### 5.4 Scenarios and stress

They need to answer:

- what happens if the front-end reprices by 25bp?
- what happens if spreads widen by 20bp?
- what happens if a prior inflation shock repeats?
- what happens under a flight-to-quality shock?

### 5.5 Data quality and market plumbing

They also need confidence that:

- curves are calibrated correctly,
- fixings are correct,
- missing data is detected,
- stale quotes are flagged,
- conventions are consistent,
- yesterday vs. today comparisons are clean.

A big part of a good macro platform is making sure the trader can trust the numbers.

## 6. Where the quant developer helps

The quant developer is not just a coder.
The quant developer turns macro reasoning into usable analytics.

## 6.1 Translate views into risk factors

The system should map macro ideas into tradable and risk-manageable factors:

- discount curves
- projection curves
- credit curves
- volatility surfaces
- FX curves
- inflation curves
- scenario factor groups

For example, a “hawkish surprise” should be expressible as a structured scenario, not only a verbal statement.

## 6.2 Build consistent market states

The developer ensures:

- quotes are normalized,
- conventions are applied,
- curves are bootstrapped consistently,
- market snapshots are versioned,
- scenario shocks are reproducible.

In system terms:

$$
\text{market data} \rightarrow \text{market state} \rightarrow \text{pricing / risk / explain}
$$

## 6.3 Provide fast recalculation

Macro desks care about speed because the data-release window is time-sensitive.
The developer helps by:

- updating handles instead of rebuilding everything,
- reusing pricing objects,
- caching market state,
- batching shocks,
- parallelizing scenario runs,
- keeping hot loops in C++.

## 6.4 Make P\&L explain credible

A macro trader needs to trust attribution.
A good explain framework separates:

$$
\Delta V = \Delta V_{\text{carry/roll}}
+ \Delta V_{\text{market moves}}
+ \Delta V_{\text{fixings/cash}}
+ \Delta V_{\text{new trades}}
+ \Delta V_{\text{residual}}
$$

This is essential because the desk wants to distinguish:

- “loss came from a genuine market move”
- versus “loss came from a bad curve build or bad market data”

## 6.5 Support how traders actually consume analytics

Typical delivery channels are:

- Python API for ad hoc analysis,
- Excel API for rapid desk workflows,
- GUI / dashboards for monitoring,
- persisted outputs for audit and comparison.

These access patterns matter because desk and control users consume analytics in different ways.

## 7. Core economic theory behind the workflow

## 7.1 Inflation

Inflation means persistent increases in the general price level.
It matters because it erodes purchasing power and changes the expected path of nominal rates.

> If inflation is above target and looks persistent, the central bank is more likely to keep policy tight. That affects
> front-end rates first, but it can propagate through the whole curve, FX, credit, and broader risk appetite.

## 7.2 Growth

Growth measures how fast the economy is expanding.
Strong growth can support profits and risk appetite, but if it is too strong it can also keep inflation high and push
rates up.

Simple explanation:

> Growth is supportive for risky assets until the market starts to worry that stronger demand will keep inflation
> elevated and force tighter policy.

## 7.3 Recession

A recession is a broad contraction in economic activity.
It usually pushes markets toward lower growth expectations, lower policy path expectations, and wider risk premia.

Simple explanation:

> In recession, sovereign duration often performs well if inflation is not the main problem, while credit and cyclical
> risk assets typically suffer because default and earnings risk increase.

## 7.4 Real rates

Real rates are approximately nominal rates minus expected inflation.
They matter because many asset valuations depend on the true cost of capital, not just nominal rates.

A useful approximation is:

$$
\text{real rate} \approx \text{nominal rate} - \text{expected inflation}
$$

## 7.5 Financial conditions

Financial conditions summarize how easy or hard it is to finance risk-taking.
Higher rates, wider spreads, stronger currency, and lower equity prices usually mean tighter financial conditions.

A good line:

> Macro is not only about GDP and CPI. It is also about how market prices feed back into the real economy through
> financial conditions.

## 8. Practical synthesis

> A global macro process can be described as a repeated cycle of data interpretation, policy-path revision, market
> repricing, trade expression, and risk monitoring. The economic layer covers growth, inflation, labor, and financial
> conditions; the market layer covers curves, spreads, FX, implied volatility, positioning, and liquidity; the platform
> layer turns that chain into consistent market states, scenarios, P&L explain, and user-facing analytics.

---

## 9. The three most important points to remember

1. Global macro is **top-down cross-asset investing**, not just long-term economics.
2. The trader focuses on **surprise versus expectation and current pricing**, not just raw data prints.
3. The quant developer adds value by making the macro view **implementable, measurable, explainable, and fast**.
