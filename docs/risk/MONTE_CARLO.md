# Monte Carlo Simulation in a Risk Engine

## 1. Why Monte Carlo is useful

Monte Carlo simulation is used when closed-form formulas are unavailable, when the portfolio contains nonlinear
instruments, or when the quantity of interest depends on the full distribution of outcomes rather than only on local
sensitivities.

Typical use cases include:

- pricing path-dependent derivatives,
- estimating portfolio loss distributions,
- computing Value at Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES),
- simulating exposures for counterparty risk,
- running model-based stress scenarios.

In a risk engine, Monte Carlo is especially useful when delta-gamma approximations are not accurate enough.

---

## 2. General idea

Suppose a portfolio value at horizon $T$ is $V_T$. Then the horizon P&L is

$$
\Pi_T = V_T - V_0.
$$

Monte Carlo estimates the distribution of $\Pi_T$ by simulating many possible future market states.

If we generate $N$ scenarios and obtain simulated values $\Pi_T^{(1)}, \dots, \Pi_T^{(N)}$, then expectations are
approximated by empirical averages:

$$
\mathbb{E}[g(\Pi_T)] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N g\left(\Pi_T^{(i)}\right).
$$

This is where the Law of Large Numbers enters: as $N \to \infty$, the empirical average converges to the true
expectation.

The Central Limit Theorem explains the statistical error:

$$
\sqrt{N}\left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N g\left(\Pi_T^{(i)}\right) - \mathbb{E}[g(\Pi_T)]\right)
\xrightarrow{d}
\mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2),
$$

where $\tau^2 = \mathrm{Var}(g(\Pi_T))$. Thus the Monte Carlo error decreases at rate $N^{-1/2}$.

## 3. Stochastic models

A generic continuous-time factor model can be written as

$$
dX_t = a(t, X_t) dt + b(t, X_t) dW_t,
$$

where:

- $X_t$ is the state variable or risk factor,
- $a(t, X_t)$ is the drift,
- $b(t, X_t)$ is the diffusion coefficient,
- $W_t$ is a Brownian motion.

Examples:

- equity under geometric Brownian motion,
- short rate under Vasicek or Hull--White,
- spread factor under a Gaussian or mean-reverting model,
- direct curve-node factors with correlated Gaussian shocks.

---

## 4. Discretization

A common numerical scheme is the Euler discretization. Over a small time step $\Delta t$, one writes

$$
X_{t+\Delta t} \approx X_t + a(t, X_t) \Delta t + b(t, X_t) \sqrt{\Delta t} Z,
$$

where

$$
Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).
$$

The factor $\sqrt{\Delta t}$ appears because Brownian increments satisfy

$$
W_{t+\Delta t} - W_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t).
$$

So one may write

$$
W_{t+\Delta t} - W_t = \sqrt{\Delta t} Z.
$$

### Example

If the short rate follows

$$
dr_t = \kappa(\theta-r_t) dt + \sigma dW_t,
$$

then Euler gives

$$
r_{t+\Delta t} \approx r_t + \kappa(\theta-r_t) \Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} Z.
$$

## 5. Correlated factors

In practice, a portfolio depends on several risk factors: rates, spreads, FX, equities, volatilities, and so on.

Let

$$
Z = (Z_1, \dots, Z_d)^\top \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d).
$$

If $\Sigma$ is the desired covariance matrix, and $L$ is a Cholesky factor such that

$$
\Sigma = LL^\top,
$$

then correlated Gaussian shocks are generated by

$$
\varepsilon = LZ.
$$

This ensures

$$
\mathrm{Cov}(\varepsilon) = \Sigma.
$$

This is the standard way to generate coherent multi-factor shocks in a first Monte Carlo implementation.

## 6. VaR and Expected Shortfall from Monte Carlo

Let simulated horizon P&Ls be

$$
\Pi^{(1)}, \dots, \Pi^{(N)}.
$$

Sort them increasingly:

$$
\Pi_{(1)} \le \Pi_{(2)} \le \cdots \le \Pi_{(N)}.
$$

For confidence level $\alpha$ (for example $0.95$), a Monte Carlo estimate of VaR is

$$
\mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha} \approx -\Pi_{(k)},
k = \lfloor (1-\alpha)N \rfloor.
$$

Expected Shortfall is estimated by averaging the tail losses:

$$
\mathrm{ES}_{\alpha} \approx -\frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k} \Pi_{(i)}.
$$

If P&L is defined as profit minus loss, then a negative value corresponds to a loss, which explains the minus signs
above.

---

## 7. Historical simulation versus model-based Monte Carlo

It is useful to distinguish three related approaches:

### 7.1. Historical simulation

Use historical factor moves directly:

$$
X_T^{(i)} = X_0 + \Delta X_{\text{hist}}^{(i)}.
$$

No parametric model is imposed.

### 7.2. Parametric Gaussian VaR

Approximate the P&L distribution analytically, often with a delta or delta-gamma approximation.

### 7.3. Model-based Monte Carlo

Specify a stochastic model for factor evolution, simulate future paths, then revalue under each path. This is the most
flexible approach, but it is also the most computationally expensive.

## 8. What the current project should implement first

For a first production-shaped implementation, the most reasonable starting point is not a full exotic path engine. It is
a coherent factor Monte Carlo around the deterministic pricing engine.

### First milestone

- define a vector of market factors,
- estimate or mock a covariance matrix,
- generate correlated Gaussian shocks,
- apply shocks to curve nodes or factor handles,
- reprice the portfolio under each scenario,
- compute portfolio P&L, VaR, and Expected Shortfall.

This gives a realistic first Monte Carlo layer for a front-office rates and macro risk platform.

### Second milestone

Add simple path dynamics, for example:

- one-factor short-rate simulation,
- spread-factor simulation,
- multi-step exposure simulation.

### Third milestone

Only after the deterministic risk and market architecture are solid, extend toward:

- path-dependent pricing,
- multi-step scenario cubes,
- variance reduction,
- parallel path generation and repricing.

---

## 9. Practical implementation concerns

### Reproducibility

Monte Carlo must support deterministic seeds. Otherwise results are hard to test and explain.

### Separation of responsibilities

A clean design separates:

- random number generation,
- factor path generation,
- scenario application,
- trade repricing,
- aggregation of portfolio metrics.

### Performance

The engine should avoid rebuilding the full market and the full instrument set for each path whenever possible. In a
QuantLib-based design, it is often more efficient to:

- construct instruments once,
- hold market quotes through handles such as `SimpleQuote`,
- mutate the quote handles per scenario,
- let QuantLib lazily update dependent instruments.

### Statistical error

Monte Carlo output should report not only point estimates but also estimation uncertainty. If $\hat m_N$ estimates a
quantity with sample standard deviation $s$, then an approximate standard error is

$$
\mathrm{SE}(\hat m_N) \approx \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}.
$$

This matters when interpreting VaR, ES, or pricing estimates from a finite number of paths.

## 10. Example: one-step factor Monte Carlo

Suppose the portfolio depends on a vector of rate and spread factors $F$. A one-step Gaussian model writes

$$
\Delta F = \mu \Delta t + LZ,
$$

where:

- $\mu$ is a drift vector,
- $L$ is a square root of the factor covariance matrix,
- $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

Then the shocked factor state is

$$
F_{t+\Delta t} = F_t + \Delta F.
$$

For each path, the engine:

1. generates $Z$,
2. computes $\Delta F$,
3. applies the shocks to the market state,
4. reprices the portfolio,
5. stores the resulting P&L.

This is simple, explainable, and directly useful in a first risk-engine implementation.

## 11. Connection with the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem

Monte Carlo relies mathematically on the same probability results used throughout statistics.

If $Y_i = g(\Pi^{(i)})$, then

$$
\hat m_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i
$$

is an estimator of $m = \mathbb{E}[Y]$.

- The **Law of Large Numbers** says $\hat m_N \to m$.
- The **Central Limit Theorem** says

$$
\sqrt{N}(\hat m_N - m)
\xrightarrow{d}
\mathcal{N}(0, \mathrm{Var}(Y)).
$$

So Monte Carlo becomes more accurate as the number of paths grows, but only at the relatively slow rate $N^{-1/2}$. That
is why variance reduction and efficient pricing infrastructure matter in practice.
